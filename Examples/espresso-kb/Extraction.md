# Extraction

**Extraction** is the process of dissolving soluble compounds from coffee grounds into water. In espresso, it occurs under pressure (typically 9 bar) and at high temperature (88–96°C), producing a concentrated beverage. The goal is to balance the extraction of desirable flavors (acids, sugars, oils) while minimizing harsh or bitter compounds.

## Under-Extraction vs. Over-Extraction

| Condition | Tasting Signs | Cause |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| **Under-extraction** | Sour, sharp, salty, weak body, thin mouthfeel, quick blonding | Too little water contact — grind too coarse, dose too low, or brew time too short |
| **Over-extraction** | Bitter, astringent, dry, hollow, ashy, harsh finish | Too much water contact — grind too fine, dose too high, or brew time too long |

**Ideal extraction** yields balanced sweetness, acidity, and body with a lingering pleasant finish.

## Key Extraction Variables

| Variable | Typical Range | Effect |
|----------|---------------|--------|
| **Grind size** | 200–400 µm (espresso) | Finer → slower flow, higher extraction; coarser → faster flow, lower extraction |
| **Dose** | 7–22 g (single/double) | More coffee → higher resistance, more solubles available |
| **Yield (output)** | 25–60 g (1:1.5–1:2.5 ratio) | Higher yield → more extraction (risk of over-extraction) |
| **Brew time** | 25–35 seconds (from first drop) | Longer time → more extraction; shorter → less |
| **Temperature** | 90–96°C | Higher temp → faster extraction, more bitterness; lower → slower, more acidity |
| **Pressure** | 8–10 bar | Higher pressure → faster flow, more extraction (within limits) |

## Practical Notes

- Adjust one variable at a time, starting with grind size.
- A 1:2 ratio (e.g., 18 g in → 36 g out in 28–32 seconds) is a common baseline.
- Taste is the final judge — numbers guide, not dictate.
